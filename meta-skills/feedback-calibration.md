# Meta-Skill: Feedback Calibration

## Domain
**Cognitive Mastery**

## Definition
Feedback Calibration is the ability to receive, process, and integrate feedback without over-reacting or dismissing it. It means separating useful signal from noise, adjusting course proportionally, and maintaining forward momentum through external input.

## Why It Matters
In teams, leaders who can't calibrate feedback either ignore critical signals or whipsaw on every comment. Both failure modes erode trust and slow progress. This skill lets you:
- Distinguish actionable signal from opinion or noise
- Adjust plans without abandoning direction
- Build a reputation as someone who actually listens — and acts wisely on it

> "The best leaders don't ignore feedback or obey it. They metabolize it."

## Example
**A product manager receives conflicting feedback after a design review.** Engineering says the scope is too large. Sales says it doesn't go far enough. The designer is frustrated by both. Instead of freezing or picking a side, she:
- Separates structural concerns (scope risk) from preference (feature wish-lists)
- Weights the engineering signal higher because it's grounded in delivery evidence
- Acknowledges the sales input, parks it for a follow-up discussion
- Adjusts scope without changing the core thesis

That's Feedback Calibration — integrating signal without distortion.

## Proficiency Levels

| Level | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **1 -- Novice** | Ignores feedback or takes all of it personally. No filter. |
| **2 -- Developing** | Listens but reacts emotionally; adjustments are disproportionate. |
| **3 -- Proficient** | Processes feedback constructively; distinguishes signal from noise. |
| **4 -- Advanced** | Actively seeks diverse feedback and integrates it systematically. |
| **5 -- Expert** | Creates feedback systems for themselves and their teams; models calibration publicly. |

## Evaluation Framework

### Behavioral Interview Questions
- "Tell me about a time you received feedback that surprised you. What did you do with it?"
- "How do you decide which feedback to act on and which to set aside?"
- "Describe a situation where conflicting feedback from different stakeholders shaped your decision."

### Scoring Rubric (1--5 Scale)
| Dimension | 1 | 3 | 5 |
|-----------|---|---|---|
| Receptiveness | Defensive or dismissive | Open when comfortable | Actively seeks disconfirming input |
| Signal Filtering | No filter — acts on everything or nothing | Filters obvious noise | Weights feedback by source credibility and evidence |
| Proportional Response | Over-corrects or ignores | Makes reasonable adjustments | Calibrates response to signal strength and context |
| Emotional Regulation | Feedback triggers shutdown or reactivity | Manages emotions post-conversation | Stays composed in the moment; processes constructively |

## Simulation Template

### Title: Post-Launch Feedback Storm

**Scenario:**
> You shipped a feature last week. Three stakeholders give you feedback: your manager says the rollout was too cautious, a peer says you moved too fast, and a customer reports a minor but visible bug. Your team is watching how you respond.

**Prompt:**
- How do you triage these three signals?
- Which do you act on immediately, and which do you park?
- How do you communicate your response to your team?

**Evaluation Tips:**
- Do they weight feedback by evidence quality, not seniority?
- Do they maintain composure and avoid defensive framing?
- Do they distinguish urgency from importance?

## Coaching Recommendations

### For Low Scores (1--2):
- Practice separating the message from the messenger
- Keep a feedback journal: log what you heard, what you felt, what you did
- Use the "24-hour rule" — no major decision changes within 24 hours of receiving feedback

### For Mid Scores (3):
- Build a personal feedback board: 3-5 trusted sources with different perspectives
- Run structured retrospectives with explicit "signal vs. noise" sorting
- Practice restating feedback before responding to confirm understanding

### For High Scores (4--5):
- Design team feedback rituals (blameless retros, pre-mortem feedback rounds)
- Teach others the distinction between feedback, opinion, and preference
- Model public calibration: share when and why you changed your mind
